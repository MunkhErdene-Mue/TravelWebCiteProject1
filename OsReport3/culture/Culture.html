NOMADIC HERITAGE

MONGOLIAN
CULTURE

Mongolian culture is rich and diverse, shaped by the country's
nomadic history, harsh environment, and unique traditions.

Nomadic Heritage

Mongolia has a long history of nomadic pastoralism, with many Mongolians traditionally herding livestock such as horses, cattle, sheep, and camels. The nomadic lifestyle has deeply influenced their culture, including their cuisine, clothing, and housing.

CUISINE

Mongolian cuisine is hearty and designed to provide sustenance in the rugged terrain. Common dishes include buuz (steamed dumplings filled with meat), khuushuur (fried meat pastries), and various forms of dairy products such as airag (fermented mare's milk).

Religion

Buddhism

Mongolia has a strong religious history, with Buddhism being the dominant religion for much of its history. Tibetan Buddhism, specifically the Gelugpa sect, is prevalent in Mongolia. Shamanism and traditional beliefs also play a significant role in the culture.

traditional dwelling

Ger or Yurt

These portable, circular tents are made from a wooden frame covered with felt and other fabrics. Yurts are easy to assemble and disassemble, making them ideal for a nomadic lifestyle.

Traditional Clothing

DEEL

It is a long, robe-like garment typically made of silk or wool and fastened with a sash. The deel is designed to protect against the harsh weather conditions of the Mongolian steppes.

Urtiin duu- traditional folk song

Long Song

The long Mongolian folk song has been registered as a UNESCO World Cultural Heritage.Typically last for hours and require great stamina from the singer. To be able to sing an urtiin duu well requires years of training and practice.

Khoomei- traditional folk song

overtone singing

Khoomei is a style of singing in which a single performer produces a diversified harmony of multiple voice parts, including a continued bass element produced in the throat.
